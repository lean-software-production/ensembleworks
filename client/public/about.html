01 / The inversion

Implementation got cheap.
Alignment didn't.

You've sat through the meeting where nothing got built. You've also watched an agent build the wrong thing, beautifully, in twelve minutes.

Coding agents flipped the economics of software work. The expensive part is no longer typing the code — it's agreeing on what "done" means precisely enough that anyone (or anything) could build it. Most teams still spend their alignment time in meetings divorced from the work, then their work time aligned with nobody.

An augmented team session fuses the two. The structures that make groups genuinely converge become the briefs that agents execute — in the same room, in the same hour, on the same canvas.

Liberating Structures

Thirty years of facilitation research distilled into tight, timeboxed formats — 1-2-4-All, Min Specs, Wise Crowds — that include every voice and force real convergence. No status theater. No loudest-person-wins.

02 / The room

A room made of distance

Spatial audio turns the canvas into a floor plan you can hear.

Inside the painted ring around any huddle, everyone is at full volume. Drift away and voices fall to a murmur — present, but not intrusive. Crew zones sit at murmur distance from each other, so you always sense the room working without being interrupted by it. Pair breakouts sit beyond earshot entirely.

One toggle — standup mode — brings the whole room to full volume. That's the facilitator's bell: plenary in one click, breakouts in one drag.

03 / The rhythm

Two burners, always lit

While the agent builds, you're already aligning on the next move.

The biggest failure mode of agent-assisted work is the slot machine: a whole team watching output scroll by. The session forbids it by architecture — your drafting table sits outside the terminal's sightline. If you're drafting, you literally can't see the stream.

Every twelve minutes, a pulse: standup mode on, one breath per crew, standup mode off. When your agent finishes, your crew swaps — review the work, launch the next brief, return to drafting. One rotating Operator stays within reach of the terminal to unblock the agent, and nothing more.

Briefs are sized to 8–15 minutes of agent work. That constraint does quiet magic: it forces the team to decompose work into small, falsifiable slices — the skill most teams never build because implementation used to be too expensive to practice it.

04 / The brief

Stickies become prompts

The wall of stickies isn't notes about the work. It is the work.

Each crew runs Min Specs — a Liberating Structure that asks one ruthless question: what are the absolute must-dos and must-not-dos? Everything else gets torn up. What survives is a short, falsifiable constraint list.

Which is, not coincidentally, the exact shape of a great agent brief. The cluster of green and red stickies gets pasted into the Claude Code terminal sitting right next to it. Minutes later there's a diff and a live preview embedded beside the brief that produced them. When the agent misreads something, the team asks which sticky failed — and the lesson lands on the shared Brief Lessons frame for every crew to steal.

By the third cycle, your team is writing measurably better specs. That skill goes home with you.

05 / The review

Review without ego —
and an agent in the crowd

The author of the code has no ego. The review can finally have none either.

Once per work block, each crew gets a Wise Crowds review: one delegate arrives from every other crew, the client crew presents the agent's work and names their doubt, consultants ask clarifying questions — then the client turns their back. They mute, drag their bubble outside the painted ring, and listen while the crowd discusses the work in the third person. Nobody defends. Everybody learns.

And there's a third consultant at every bench: a reviewer agent reading the same diff, briefed to return exactly three sticky-sized lines — one risk, one simplification, one missing test. Its advice goes up anonymously, same yellow as everyone else's. The client keeps what's useful, on merit alone.

At the closing debrief, the agent's stickies are revealed. Over a few sessions, your team builds something rare: a calibrated sense of where agent judgment is strong and where it's noise.

The kept advice doesn't annotate the diff — it feeds the next brief. The consultants advise the spec; the agent does the rework.

06 / The session

100 minutes, end to end

Every step is a Liberating Structure with a hard timebox. Every artifact lands on the canvas. Nothing is a slide.

0:00
9 Whys · in pairs

Why this session?

Pairs drift to huddles beyond earshot and dig past the surface goal. Purpose stickies converge at the center.

0:10
1-2-4-All

What could we build?

Alone in your own corner, then pairs, then standup mode for the full room. Every voice lands a sticky before any voice dominates.

0:25
25/10 Crowd Sourcing

Converge

The room ranks the ideas. The top two or three become workstreams — and crews form around them.

0:35
Min Specs

Sharpen the first briefs

Each crew tears its workstream down to must-dos and must-not-dos. Green stickies, red stickies, nothing vague survives.

0:45
Two burners · Wise Crowds at each pulse

The work block

Agents build on crew branches while humans draft the next briefs. Pulses every 12 minutes. Delegates rotate through reviews. Brief lessons accumulate at the center of the room.

1:30
Shift & Share

Demos

The whole room travels bench to bench. The demo is the live app in an embedded frame — not a screenshot, not a promise.

1:40
What, So What, Now What

Debrief — and the reveal

Which advice stickies were the agent's? What did we learn about briefing? Now-What stickies seed the next session's backlog.
